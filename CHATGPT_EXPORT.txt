## Slide 2 — How Work Traditionally Happens

---

### STEP 0 · Slide 2 · ~30 sec

#### SAY:

Let's start with how work actually happens in most businesses today — including mine, and probably yours.

---

### STEP 1 · Slide 2 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

It starts with your business systems. QuickBooks. Your email. Maybe a CRM or scheduling tool. Your point-of-sale system. The information lives there — customer records, invoices, job history, inventory.

---

### STEP 2 · Slide 2 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

But the systems don't talk to each other. So who connects them? You. Your office manager. Your bookkeeper. Your best employee who "just knows where everything is."

Humans are the connective tissue between every piece of software in your business.

---

### STEP 3 · Slide 2 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

And what does that look like day to day? PDFs. Excel spreadsheets. Word documents. Email chains.

These are the artifacts of knowledge work — the reports, proposals, recaps, and updates that move between people.

---

### STEP 4 · Slide 2 · ~45 sec

#### SAY:

Information is constantly in motion. Pulled from one system, pasted into a spreadsheet, emailed to someone, imported into another tool.

Every handoff takes time. Every handoff adds a chance someone misreads something or uses an outdated version.

---

### STEP 5 · Slide 2 · ~45 sec

#### SAY:

Reports get assembled manually — someone exports data, someone else formats it, someone else checks the numbers. And then you meet.

Why do we have so many meetings? Because the information is scattered. Meetings exist to synchronize what everyone should already know.

---

### STEP 6 · Slide 2 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

And finally — decisions. But decisions wait. They wait on the report that took three days to assemble. They wait on the person who has the full picture. They wait on the meeting that gets scheduled for next Tuesday.

Does this sound familiar to anyone?

#### PAUSE

---

### STEP 7 · Slide 2 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

Here's the insight I want you to hold onto: in most businesses, humans spend an enormous amount of time moving information around — not creating value, not serving customers, not making strategic decisions. Just moving information. Copying, pasting, exporting, emailing, reformatting.

So what changes? That's where AI comes in — but not in the way most people think.

---

## Slide 3 — AI Moves Up The Value Chain

---

### STEP 0 · Slide 3 · ~30 sec

#### SAY:

AI is not new. But its role in work is fundamentally shifting. To understand where we're headed, it helps to see how tools have evolved over time.

---

### STEP 1 · Slide 3 · ~45 sec

#### SAY:

We started with calculators. Tools that assist computation. You do the thinking; the tool does the math.

---

### STEP 2 · Slide 3 · ~45 sec

#### SAY:

Then software. QuickBooks stores your books. Your CRM stores your customers. Spreadsheets store your data. Software organizes and stores — but you still do all the thinking and moving.

---

### STEP 3 · Slide 3 · ~45 sec

#### SAY:

Automation came next. If-this-then-that. Rules. "When a form is submitted, send an email." "When inventory drops below 10, reorder." Repeatable, rule-based tasks. Useful — but rigid. It can't handle ambiguity.

---

### STEP 4 · Slide 3 · ~45 sec

#### SAY:

Now AI. And this is the shift. AI can reason over unstructured information — emails, documents, notes, messy spreadsheets. It doesn't just store data or follow rules. It interprets.

---

### STEP 5 · Slide 3 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

And the newest layer: agents. An agent doesn't just respond to a prompt — it pursues a goal. It thinks, acts, checks the result, and tries again.

AI can now participate in work — not just store information, not just follow rules, but actively contribute to getting things done.

What does that actually look like? Let me show you the mental model.

---

## Slide 4 — The Agentic Loop

---

### STEP 0 · Slide 4 · ~30 sec

#### SAY:

This is the mental model that makes AI feel different from every tool you've used before. I call it the agentic loop.

---

### STEP 1 · Slide 4 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

An agent starts with a goal — not a single question, but an outcome. "Get me a summary of overdue invoices by customer."

Then it thinks: what do I need to do? It acts: query the system, read the data. It observes: did I get what I need? If not, it thinks again and acts again.

Goal, think, act, observe — in a loop until the job is done.

---

### STEP 2 · Slide 4 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

Compare that to traditional software. Input in, output out. One pass. Fixed result.

You ask QuickBooks for a report, you get the report it was designed to give you. If that's not the right report, you figure it out yourself.

---

### STEP 3 · Slide 4 · ~1 min

#### SAY:

An agent operates differently. Goal, iterate, complete.

Watch the loop — each iteration refines the approach. The iteration counter is going up because it's not done in one pass. It's working the problem.

#### PAUSE — let animation run through 2–3 iterations

---

### STEP 4 · Slide 4 · ~45 sec

## Q&A — Exact Answers

---

**"Will AI replace my employees?"**

#### SAY:

No — and the businesses I see winning with AI aren't cutting headcount. They're doing more with the same team. The office manager who spent half her day copying data between systems now spends that time on client relationships. AI removes the tedious connective-tissue work, not the people.

---

**"I'm not technical. Can I actually use this?"**

#### SAY:

That's exactly what the QuickBooks demo was about. I described a business question in plain English. I didn't write code — AI did. Your starting point isn't learning to code. It's identifying one repetitive task in your business and asking whether AI could handle it.

---

**"How much does this cost?"**

#### SAY:

ChatGPT Plus is twenty dollars a month per user. Many tools have free tiers. The bigger investment is time — figuring out where AI fits in your workflow. On the custom side, connecting AI to your systems varies widely, but you don't need to start there. Start with one person, one use case, one month of experimenting.

---

**"Is my business data safe?"**

#### SAY:

Valid concern. Generic ChatGPT uses your inputs for training unless you opt out. Business plans and enterprise tools offer data controls. When you connect AI to QuickBooks or your CRM, you control what access it has — same as any other software integration. Be intentional. Don't paste sensitive data into free tools without checking the terms.

---

**"I tried ChatGPT and it wasn't that useful."**

#### SAY:

That's the most common thing I hear. Generic chat is like a brilliant intern who doesn't know your business. Ask it to write a marketing email and you get something generic. Connect it to your customer data, your past proposals, your pricing — and it becomes a different tool entirely. The demo today was about that shift.

---

**"Where should I start?"**

#### SAY:

Pick one task someone on your team does every week that involves moving information — summarizing emails, building a report, drafting a proposal. Have them try doing it with ChatGPT or Claude for two weeks. One task, one person. See what changes. Don't try to transform the whole business at once.

---

**"Does this work for my industry?"**

#### SAY:

The pattern is universal: information lives in systems, humans move it, AI can help. The specifics depend on your tools and workflows. Once I know your industry, I can point to a concrete starting point — job estimates for trades, client intake for professional services, inventory reports for retail.

---

**"Do I need to hire someone like you?"**

#### SAY:

Not to start. You can experiment on your own with the tools available today. Where companies like mine come in is when you want AI connected to your systems — automated workflows, custom agents, integrations with QuickBooks, CRM, email. That's when the productivity gains compound. But the first step is free and you can do it this week.

---

**"What about accuracy? AI makes things up."**

#### SAY:

It does — and you should always review output, especially for client-facing or financial work. Think of AI as a fast draft, not a final answer. The agentic loop helps — it checks and iterates — but human judgment stays in the loop. That's the "move up the value chain" idea.

---

**"How fast is this moving? Should I wait?"**

#### SAY:

The businesses experimenting now are building institutional knowledge about how AI fits their operations. The ones who wait won't lose overnight — but they'll be learning while competitors are compounding. You don't need to go all-in. You need to start.
