## Slide 2 — How Work Still Gets Done

So before we dive into what AI looks like today and how it is starting to improve businesses, I want to take a step back and talk for a minute about how businesses have traditionally operated — and honestly, how the vast majority of businesses still operate today.

### Systems of record

At the foundation, every business has **systems of record**. These are the places where information lives and gets collected over time.

That could be your accounting system, your email inbox, your CRM, shared documents or spreadsheets your team relies on, or some industry-specific platform that runs part of the business.

These systems hold the raw ingredients of the business. Things like customer info, invoices, orders, inventory — basically all info that digitally lives in your company. 

But in most businesses, those systems do not naturally understand each other, and they do not independently turn that information into useful action.

### The human connection layer

So in the middle, we have what I would call the **human connection layer**.

These are the people who know how the business actually works and who are responsible for the processes that connect all of those systems together.

- It might be an office manager pulling information from a scheduling system and updating a spreadsheet.
- It might be a salesperson reviewing notes in the CRM, building a quote, and sending it to a customer.
- It might be a bookkeeper reconciling information between a bank account, an accounting system, invoices, and emails.

The important point is that **humans are often the glue**. They gather information, they interpret it, they move it from point A to point B, and ultimately are the ones that decide what should happen next.

### Artifacts and actions

And then, as a result of that human layer, we get what I think of as **artifacts and actions**.

These are the actual outputs that move the business forward: reports being created, quotes being sent, emails being exchanged, orders being placed, all of that data movement that determines a business's productivity. 

So overall, at least from a digital perspective, this is a pretty good representation of how a lot of businesses operate:

And I want you to keep this model in mind as we go through the rest of the presentation, because this is exactly where AI is starting to change the way work gets done.

## Slide 3 — AI Is Entering the Human Connection Layer

So this is where things start to get interesting.

**AI is beginning to take over parts of that connection layer.**

It can read information from your systems, interpret context, compare different sources, draft an output, and take action through the tools your business already uses.

And when you have that ability, it creates a very different model of work.

- Sometimes the AI can take a piece of information all the way through to action on its own.
- Other times, it can do most of the work, recognize that human judgment is needed, pass the relevant information to a person for review or approval, and then continue the process from there.

That is what people mean when they talk about **human-in-the-loop AI**.

The human is still involved — but they are no longer responsible for manually carrying every piece of information through the process.

Instead, the AI becomes more of the **orchestration layer**. It handles the movement, the interpretation, and a lot of the execution, while only bringing people in where more judgment, context, or accountability is actually needed.

And to me, this is one of the biggest shifts happening right now and reeally one of the most practical ways AI is being applied. 

It's really moving from something you occasionally ask a question to, into something that can actively participate in the flow of work itself.

## Slide 4 — From Instructions to Outcomes

So the natural question is: what actually makes AI capable of doing this?

Why can it suddenly sit in that middle layer, work with information from different systems, and help move a process forward?

And I think the simplest way to explain it is that modern AI does not always operate like traditional software.

### Traditional software

Traditional software is generally very linear. You give it an input, it follows a predefined process, and it gives you an output.

- You click a button to run a report.
- You fill out a form and an email gets sent.
- A certain condition happens and a predefined automation runs.

And that model is still incredibly useful. But it is fixed. The software is following a path that someone already defined.

### AI agents

**AI agents can operate differently.**

Instead of only giving the system a specific instruction, you can give it a **goal**.

> **Example goal:** Identify which customers are seriously overdue and recommend what we should do next.

The AI can then work through that problem:

1. It might first think, *"I need invoice data."* → So it checks the accounting system.
2. Then it observes what it found and realizes that several customers are overdue.
3. But maybe that is not enough context. So now it checks CRM notes or recent email history.
4. It might discover that one customer simply needs a reminder, while another has an active service issue and probably should not receive an automated collections email.
5. So it adjusts.
   - One case might move directly toward an action.
   - Another might get routed to a person for review.

And this process can continue until the goal is complete.

That is the basic idea behind what people call the **agentic loop**:

> **Think → Act → Observe → Adjust → Continue**

And I think this is one of the biggest reasons AI feels so different from traditional software.

| Traditional software | AI agents |
|---|---|
| Follows instructions | Works toward an outcome |

## Slide 5 — Outputs Are Becoming Dynamic and Interactive

So one of the places I think this becomes really visible is in the actual outputs businesses create.

For a long time, most business artifacts have been pretty fixed.

- An data analysis report is an Excel workbook.
- A proposal is usually a PDF.
- A meeting summary is usually a Word document 

And again, there is nothing wrong with any of those formats.

But what I think is changing is how easy it is to create something much more flexible around the same underlying information.

A spreadsheet can become a custom dashboard with filters, drill-downs, recommendations, and different ways to explore the data.

A proposal can become a web-based experience where the client selects options, sees pricing change in real time, and directly accept the proposal right then and there. 

Historically, building things like that took a lot of time and a lot of technical skill. And often times it was very expensive. 

But AI is starting to collapse that gap.

It is becoming much easier to take the same underlying business information and turn it into something dynamic, interactive, and tailored to the person using it.
